Overview

The liver plays a key role in the maintenance of blood glucose
homeostasis. It releases glucose in the fasted state and takes
up and stores some of the glucose ingested in meals. In doing
so the liver ensures an even and predictable supply of glucose
to the extrahepatic tissues, primarily the brain.

The liver is able to release glucose to the circulation from
its glycogen stores (glycogenolysis) and it has the capacity
to take up the 3-carbon fragments lactate, pyruvate, glycerol
and amino acids and convert these to glucose. The latter process
is called gluconeogenesis.

In the overnight fasted state, the liver is the main source of
glucose production and it is widely believed that glycogenolysis
and gluconeogenesis each contribute approximately 50% of glucose
turnover in healthy subjects.

During continued fasting,gluconeogenesis from both the kidneys
and the liver account for almost all glucose production.
