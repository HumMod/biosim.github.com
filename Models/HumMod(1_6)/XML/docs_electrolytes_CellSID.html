<topicdoc>
<name> CellSID - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Peter Stewart was a Canadian physiologist who introduced
the term "strong ion difference" or [SID]. SID in any
solution is defined as the sum of all the strong cation
concentrations minus the sum of all the strong anion
concentrations.

The strong cations in cells are mainly potassium (K+),
with small amounts of sodium(Na+) and magnesium (Mg++).
Strong anions are phosphate ions and other inorganic ions.

Stewart's original estimate of of cell SID at 0.130 is
rather high. Experimental evidence suggests a rather
low cell SID. This evidence is the pH changes which occur
when CO2 and acid microinjection have been used to acidify
the cell. Additionally, the pH changes due to lactate
generation during exercise support a low cell SID.

control. Respir Physiol 33: 9�26, 1978.

<topicdoc>
<name> OtherCations - DefinitionCS2 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

The main cation contributing to cell SID is potassium.
All other cations are defined as other cations and include
magnesium and sodium. The contribution of these latter two
cations to CellSID is rather small - 0.012mEq/L.

SID = KCell.[K+] + OtherCations - StrongAnions
