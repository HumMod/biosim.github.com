<topicdoc>
<name> Glycogen - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

After absorption into a cell, glucose can be used immediately for
release of energy to the cell, or it can be stored in the form of
glycogen, a large polymer of glucose. Liver cells and skeletal
muscle cells can store large amounts of glycogen. The glycogen
molecules can be polymerized to almost any molecular weight and
most of the glycogen precipitates in the form of solid granules.

The process of glycogen formation is called glycogenesis.

The process of glycogen breakdown is called glycogenolysis.

The factors that control glycogen synthesis are insulin, blood
glucose and the size of the glycogen pool.

Kruszynska YT. Normal metabolism: the physiology of fuel
homeostasis. Chapter 9, Textbook of Diabetes edited by
Pickup JC & Williams G. 2003 Blackwell Science

</content>
</topicdoc>

<name> Change - DefinitionGLY </name>
<label> Defintion </label>
<content>

Changes in the glycogen mass are a function of both gains and
losses in glycogen integrated over time.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Mass - DefinitionGLY </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

The mass of glycogen is the amount of glycogen present in the
liver at a given point. It is a function of gain and loss of
glycogen over time.

</content>
</topicdoc>


<topicdoc>
<name> Gain - DefinitionGLY </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

The gain in glycogen mass is defined as the positive change
in glycogen mass and is a function of the rate of glycogenesis.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Loss - DefinitionGLY </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

The loss in glycogen mass is defined as the negative change
in glycogen mass and is a function of rate of glycogenolysis.

</content>
</topicdoc>
