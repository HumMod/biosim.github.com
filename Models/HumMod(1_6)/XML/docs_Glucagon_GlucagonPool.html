<topicdoc>
<name> Glucagon - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Glucagon is a single long-chain polypeptide secreted
by the alpha cells of the pancreatic islets of Langerhans.
It plays an important role in glucose homeostasis.
It is released into the hepatic portal circulation in
response to decreases in plasma glucose levels.
Glucagon stimulates gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis
in the liver. Both lead to an enhanced availability of
glucose. At the same time, it decreases glycogenesis
and glycolysis

Glucagon is the main counterregulatory hormone to insulin
and the insulin to glucagon ratio determines the control
of gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis. Glucagon increases
the hepatic uptake of precursors, especially amino acids,
and their conversion into glucose.

Kruszynska YT. Normal metabolism: the physiology of fuel
homeostasis. Chapter 9, Textbook of Diabetes edited by
Pickup JC & Williams G. 2003 Blackwell Science

Queseda I, Tuduri E, Ripoll C, Nadal A. Physiology of
the pancreatic alpha cell and glucagon secretion: role
in glucose homeostasis and diabetes.
J Endocrinology 199:5-19, 2008.

Gromada J, Franklin I, Wollheim CB. Alpha cells of the
endocrine pancreas: 35 years of research but the enigma
remains. Endocrine reviews 28: 84 -116, 2007.

</content>

<topicdoc>
<name> Glucagon - Concentration Units </name>
<label> Concentration Units </label>
<content>

Molecular Weight (MW) of glucagon is 3550

Typical plasma glucagon concentration [Glucagon] = 70 pG/mL
The volume of distribution is the ECFV.

Glucagon Mass = ECFV * [Glucagon]
= 15000 * 70 = 1,050,000 pG or 1,050 nG

Typical glucagon concentrations under a variety of
physiological situations


Starve 300pG/mL
Fast 100pG/mL
Drip 70pG/mL
Meal 50pG/mL
Load 40pG/mL

Unger RH, Aguilar-Parada E, Muller WA, Eisentrant AM.
Studies of pancreatic alpha cell function in normal
and diabetic patients. J. Clin Invest. 49: 837-848, 1970

Felig P, Wahren J, Sherwin R, Hendler R. Insulin, glucagon,
and somatostatin in normal physiology and diabetes mellitus
Diabetes 25: 1091 - 1099, 1976

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Gain - Definition1 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Gain is defined as positive changes in mass of glucagon
and is a function of pancreatic secretion rate of glucagon.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Change - Definition1 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

The rate of change of glucagon mass is the gain of glucagon
minus the loss of glucagon. The rate is integrated over time
and divided by the ECFV to calculate plasma concentration
at any given time.

</content>
</topicdoc>


<topicdoc>
<name> InitialMass - Definition1 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

The initial mass is the starting amount of glucagon.
It is the initial ECFV * Target[Glucagon]. The initial
ECFV is affected by body traits (size, adiposity and
muscularity). Target[Glucagon] is the starting plasma
glucagon concentration.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Loss - Definition1 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Loss is defined as negative changes in mass of glucagon.
It is a function of glucagon clearance.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Mass - Definition1 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Mass is the amount of glucagon at any given time.
It is a function of both gains and losses in
glucagon over time. Gain is a function of pancreatic
secretion rate and loss is a function of glucagon
clearance rate.

</content>
</topicdoc>
<topicdoc>
<name> Target[Glucagon] - Definition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Target[Glucagon] is the starting or initial plasma
glucagon concentration.

</content>
</topicdoc>
