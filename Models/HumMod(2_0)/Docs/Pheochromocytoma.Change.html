Overview

Pheochromocytomas are rare catecholamine-secreting tumors
derived from the chromaffin cells of the embryonic neural
crest. In 1912, the pathologist Ludwig Pick formulated the
term pheochromocytoma, which decribes the colorful reaction
of tumor cells in response to the addition of fixatives
containing chromic acid salts.
Ninety percent of tumors occur in the adrenal medulla;
the remainder are extrarenal and are known as secreting
paragangliomas. In most patients with pheochromocytomas,
plasma catecholamine concentrations are elevated. Unlike the
healthy adrenal medulla, however, pheochromocytomas are not
innervated, and catecholamine release is not precipitated by
neural stimulation.

Most pheochromocytomas secrete both norepinephrine and
epinephrine although norepinephrine predominates. Tumors
can be both only norepinephrine or epinephrine secreting,
although the latter is very rare. An increase in the production
of catecholamines causes symptoms which include hypertension,
severe headaches, palpitations and excess sweating. These
symptoms reflect the effects of epinephrine and norepinephrine
on alpha and beta adrenergic receptors respectively.
Some pheochromocytomas secrete catecholamines intermittently
and cause paroxysmal hypertension; others cause sustained
hypertension by constantly secreting catecholamines.
