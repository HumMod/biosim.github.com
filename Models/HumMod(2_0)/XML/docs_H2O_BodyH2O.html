<topicdoc>
<name> BodyH20 - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

There are several different ways to describe the body's
water compartments.Water compartments can initially be
divided into two basic types: intracellular (ICFV) and
extracellular (ECFV).

Intracellular compartments are red blood cells and
(all other) cells.

Extracellular compartments are plasma and interstitium.

The problem with this scheme is that water cannot move
freely between plasma and interstitium but it can move
freely between interstitium and cell H2O.

Another way of dividing the body's water compartments
is into intravascular and extravascular compartments.
The intravascular compartment can be futher subdivided
into RBC water and plasma. Extravascular water is also
referred to as tissue H2O.

Changes in body water may occur as result of both
internal and external exchanges.Internal exchanges
are capillary filtration and lymph flow.External
exchanges are many and include absorption
from gut, urine, IV drip, transfusion,
hemorrhage ...

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Gain - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Gains in body water may occur as a result of
increased absorption from the gut; decreases
in urine output; IV drip or transfusion.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Loss - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Losses in body water may occur as a result of
decreased absorption from the gut; increases in
urine output; vomiting and hemorrhage.

</content>
</topicdoc>
