<topicdoc>
<name> Baroreflex - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

The baroreceptor reflex has three parts: an afferent
part, processing in the central nervous system, and an
efferent part. The afferent part of the baroreceptor
reflex consists of nerve endings that act as pressure
sensors in the arch of the aorta and carotid arteries.
Receptors are most concentrated along the carotid artery
in a region called the carotid sinus. Increasing pressure
is excitatory and signals are transmitted from each
carotid sinus through very small Hering's nerves to the
glossopharyngeal nerve and then to the tractus solitarius
in the medullary area of the brain stem. Signals from the
aortic baroreceptors in the arch of the aorta are transmitted
through the vagus nerve to the same tractus solitarius of the
medulla. The baroreceptors respond extremely rapidly
to changes in arterial pressure.

Angel-Davis J, Daly M de B. Comparison of reflex
vasomotor responses to separate and combined stimulation
of carotid sinus and aortic arch baroreceptors by
pulsatile and non-pulsatile pressures in the dog.
J Physiol 209: 257-293, 1970.


</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> AdaptedPressure - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

The acute response of the baroreceptors to a blood
pressure change is much stronger than the eventual
response. In other words, the baroreceptors adapt over
time to the prevailing pressure.


</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> NA - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Nerve activity(NA) is quantified in multiples of normal
activity. Afferent nerve activity is one times normal
when the stimulating pressure is zero. A rising pressure
increases afferent nerve activity while a falling pressure
decreases it.

</content>
</topicdoc>
