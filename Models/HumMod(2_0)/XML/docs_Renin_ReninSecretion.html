<topicdoc>
<name> TGFEffect - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Tubuloglomerular feedback (TGF) is the process whereby
the macula densa cells of the kidney regulate glomerular
filtration rate (GFR). The macula densa cells are
morphologically distinct distal tubular cells that come
in contact with the juxtaglomerular cells in the walls
of the afferent and efferent arterioles. The macula
densa cells sense changes in distal tubule volume delivery
and composition. A decrease in GFR results in a
reduction in sodium chloride concentration at the macula
densa cells. This initiates a signal from these specialized
cells that has two effects: (1) It decreases resistance to
flow in the afferent arterioles, which raises glomerular
hydrostatic pressure and helps return GFR to normal and (2)
it increases renin release resulting in increased
formation of angiotensin II which constricts the efferent
arterioles and helps return GFR toward normal.

Macula Densa cells detect changes in luminal sodium chloride
concentration through a complex series of ion transport-
related intracellular events. NaCl entry via a Na:K:2Cl
cotransporter mechanism and Cl exit through a basolateral
channel lead to cell depolarization and increases in cytosolic
calcium. An increase in cytosolic calcium attenuates the release
of renin, likely by modulating cytosolic cAMP concentration.

In HumMod, the effect of TGF on renin release is expressed
as a multiplier of base secretion.
