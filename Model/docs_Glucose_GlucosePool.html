<topicdoc>
<name> Glucose - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Glucose is the final product of carbohydrate digestion and is
used as a fuel source for the production of ATP in the cells
of the body. Glucose is the principal fuel source for the brain
and cerebral function is critically dependent on maintaining
plasma glucose levels within narrow limits.
Plasma glucose levels depend on the rates of entry of glucose
into the circulation and of its uptake into the tissues.
The main tissues that regulate blood glucose levels are the
liver (by glucose production and uptake) and skeletal muscle
(glucose uptake). The hormones insulin and glucagon function
as important feedback control systems for maintaining a normal
blood glucose concentration.

Immediately after a high-carbohydrate meal, the glucose that
is absorbed into the blood causes an increase in plasma
concentration of glucose and subsequent rapid secretion of
insulin. Insulin causes rapid uptake and storage of glucose
by almost all tissues of the body but especially by the muscles,
adipose tissue, and liver. Excess glucose is stored in the
form of glycogen. Insulin also inhibits glycogen breakdown
and glucose release by the liver. Between meals, when
food is not available and the blood glucose concentration
begins to fall, insulin secretion decreases rapidly and
secretion of glucagon increases. Liver glycogen is split back
into glucose (glycogenolysis) and released back into the
blood to maintain the plasma glucose levels. The decrease
in insulin secretion and increase in glucagon release also
causes increased gluconeogenesis in the liver and results in
the breakdown and use of stored glucose, fats and proteins.
Both of these effects greatly enhance the availability of
glucose to other organs of the body.

Kruszynska YT. Normal metabolism: the physiology of fuel
homeostasis. Textbook of Diabetes. Chapter 9. Edited by
Pickup JC & Willams G. Third Edition 2003. Blackwell Science.

Cryer PE. Glucose Counterregulation: prevention and correction
of hypoglycemia in humans. Am J Physiol 264: E149 - E155, 1993


</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Glucose - Physical Units </name>
<label> Physical Units </label>
<content>

Plasma Concentration mG/mL, mG/dL or nMol/L
Mass mG
mG/dL * 0.05551 = mMol/L
mMol * 0.182 = mG
mG * 5.551 = mMol
mG/mL * 100 = mG/dL

</content>
</topicdoc>


<topicdoc>
<name> Glucose - Concentration Units1 </name>
<label> Concentration Units </label>
<content>

Molecular Weight (MW) of glucose is 180

Typical plasma glucose concentration [Glucose] = 1.1mg/mL
The volume of distribution is the ECFV.

Glucose Mass = ECFV * [Glucose]
= 15000 * 1.1 = 16500mG

Typical glucose concentrations under a variety of
physiological situations


Hypoglycemia 45 mG/dL ; 2.5 mMol/L
Starving 60 mG/dL ; 3.3 mMol/L
Fasting 85 mG/dL ; 4.7 mMol/L
Drip 110 mG/dL ; 6.1 mMol/L
Fed 130 mG/dL ; 7.2 mMol/L
Glucose Load 200 mG/dL ; 11.1 mMol/L

</content>
</topicdoc>

<name> Change - GPDefinition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Change in glucose mass is the gain in glucose minus the loss
in glucose.

</content>
</topicdoc>


<topicdoc>
<name> Gain - GPDefinition2 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Gain is defined as positive changes in mass of glucose
and is a function of GI lumen carbohydrate absorption rate,
rate of liver release of glucose and infused glucose (using
the glucose pump feature).

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Loss - GPDefinition3 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Loss is defined as negative changes in mass of glucose
and is a function of uptake of glucose into the cells
of the body; liver uptake; loss of glucose in the kidney
(Nephron Glucose spillover) and glucose decomposition rate.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Osmolarity - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Osmolarity is the osmolal concentration of a solution
expressed as osmoles (osm) per liter of solution. One
osmole equal to one mole (mol)(6.02 * 10^23) of solute
particles. Therefore a solution containing 1 mole of
glucose in each liter has a concentration of 1 osm/L.
In general, the osmole is too large a unit for expressing
osmotic activity of solutes in body fluids. The term
milliosmole (mOsm), which equals 1/1000 osmole, is
commonly used.

A normal plasma concentration of glucose is 100mG/dL.
100mG/dL = 1000mG/L. The MW of glucose is 180
Therefore 100mG/dL = 1000/180 = 5.55 mOsm/L.

In patients with diabetes, plasma glucose levels can
rise to dangerously high levels. This can exert a
large amount of osmotic pressure in the extracellular
fluid and result in cellular dehydration. Increased
excretion of glucose in the urine can also cause osmotic
diuresis and a depletion of body fluids.

Guyton AC, Hall JE. Textbook of medical physiology.
Eleventh Edition. Elsevier Saunders.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> InitialMass - GPDefinition4 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

The initial mass is the starting amount of glucose.
It is the initial ECFV * Target[Glucose]. The initial
ECFV is affected by body traits (size, adiposity and
muscularity). Target[Glucose] is the starting plasma
glucose concentration.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Mass - GPDefinition5 </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Changes in the glucose pool or mass are a function of both
gains and losses in glucose over time. Gain is a function
of GI lumen carbohydrate absorption rate,
rate of liver release of glucose and infused glucose (using
the glucose pump feature). Loss is a function of uptake
of glucose into the cells of the body; liver uptake; loss
of glucose in the kidney (Nephron Glucose Spillover)
and glucose decomposition rate.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<name> Target[Glucose] - GPDefinition6 </name>

<content>

Target[Glucose] is the starting or initial plasma
