<topicdoc>
<name> Epinephrine - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

In 1895, Oliver and Schaefer first showed that a substance
present in the adrenal gland was pressor. This substance was
subsequently shown to be epinephrine.

Oliver G, Schaefer EA. The physiologic effects of extracts
from the suprarenal capsules. J Physiol 18:230-276, 1895

Barger G, Dale HH. Chemical structure and sympathomimetic
action of amines. J Physiol 41:19-59, 1910.

Stimulation of the sympathetic nerves to the adrenal
medullae causes release of both epinephrine and
norepinephrine into the circulation. Catecholamines
exert their effects by stimulating specific protein
receptor sites known as adrenergic receptors. There are
two types of adrenergic receptors, alpha and beta receptors.
Epinephrine excites both types of receptors.

The epinephrine pool or mass is a function of epinephrine
secretion, epinephrine clearance and the volume of distribution
which is the ECFV.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> [Epi] - Concentration Units </name>
<label> Concentration Units </label>
<content>

Epinephrine (C9 H13 N O3) has a molecular weight
of 183. Typical plasma concentration is 40pG/mL.
or 0.22nMol/L (Multiply by 0.00546 to convert pG/mL
to nMol/L).

Fitzgerald GA, Barnes P, Hamilton CA, Dollery CT
Circulating adrenaline and blood pressure: the
metabolic effects and kinetics of infused adrenaline
in man. Eur J Clin Invest 10: 401-406, 1980

Cohen G, Holland B, Sha J and Goldenberg M
Plasma concentrations of epinephrine and norepinephrine
during intravenous infusions in man.
J Clin Invest 38:1935-1941, 1959

Clutter WE, Bier DM, Shah SD and Cryer PE
Epinephrine plasma metabolic clearance rates and
physiologic thresholds for metabolic and hemodynamic
actions in man.
J Clin Invest 66: 94-101, 1980

Stratton JR, Pfeifer MA, Ritchie LJ and Halter JB
Hemodynamic effects of epinephrine: concentration-effect
study in humans.
J Appl Physiol 58(4):1199-1206, 1985

Tidgren B and Hjemdahl P. Renal responses to mental
stress and epinephrine in humans.
Am J Physiol 257:F682-F689, 1989

Goldstein DS, Eisenhofer GF, Kopin IJ
Sources and significance of plasma levels of catechols
and their metabolites in humans.
J Pharmacol Exp Ther 305(3):800-811, 2003

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Change - EP1Definition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Changes in the Epi pool or mass are a function of both
gains and losses in epinephrine over time. Gains may
occur from increased secretion due to increased sympathetic
nerve stimulation; from infusion of epinephrine
using the epi pump feature of the model and/or increases
from a pheochromocytoma.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> F1 - EP2Definition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

F1 is a mathematical variable used in the calculation of
the mass of Epi. F1 is the gain in Epi over time and is
a function of Epi secretion rate, Epi pump rate and
pheochromocytoma Epi rate.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> F2 - EP3Definition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

F2 is a mathematical variable used in the calculation of
the mass of Epi. F2 is the loss in Epi over time and is
a function of Epi clearance rate.

</content>
</topicdoc>


<topicdoc>
<name> Gain - EP4Definition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Gain is defined as positive changes in mass of epinephrine
and is a function of epinephrine secretion rate,
epinephrine from infusion (using the Epi pump feature) and
any epinephrine secretion from a possible pheochromocytoma.
The model user may create a pheochromocytoma and
define different levels of severity.

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Loss - EP6Definition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Loss is defined as a negative change in mass of epinephrine
and is a function of clearance rate.

Goldstein DS, Eisenhofer G, Kopin IJ. Sources and
significance of plasma levels of catechols and their
metabolites in humans.
J Pharmacol Exp Ther 305(3):800-811, 2003

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> InitialMass - EP5Definition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

The initial mass is the starting amount of Epi. Since the
plasma concentration is 40pG/mL (the target[Epi]) and the
volume of distribution is the ECFV (15,000mL), then the
initial mass or circulating pool is 600nG or 600,000pG.

Initial Mass = Target[Epi]*ECFV.Vol

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Mass - EP7Definition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

Mass is the calculated amount of epinephrine at any
any given time. Change in the Epi pool or mass is a
function of both gains and losses in epinephrine over time.
Gains may occur from increased secretion due to increased
sympathetic nerve stimulation; from infusion of epinephrine
using the epi pump feature of the model and/or increases
from a pheochromocytoma.
Changes in the pool are calculated in the model using two
mathematical variables, F1 and F2, which integrate gains and
losses over time. A Backward Euler integral is used in this
calculation because it gives a more stable solution set.

Fitzgerald GA, Barnes P, Hamilton CA, Dollery CT.
Circulating Adrenaline and blood pressure: the
metabolic effects and kinetics of infused adrenaline
in man. Eur J Clin Invest 10: 401-406, 1980.

Goldstein DS, Eisenhofer G, Kopin IJ. Sources and
significance of plasma levels of catechols and their
metabolites in humans.
J Pharmacol Exp Ther 305(3):800-811, 2003

</content>
</topicdoc>

<topicdoc>
<name> Target[Epi] - EP8Definition </name>
<label> Definition </label>
<content>

During startup, the model user defines the particular
traits of the HumMod patient. These are gender, body size,
age, muscularity and adiposity. All of these traits
influence body volumes, most notably the extracellular
fluid volume (ECFV). The assumption is made that
while volumes may change with traits, concentrations do
not. At startup,initial volumes,especially ECFV and plasma
volume are calculated. A target concentration is specified
- in this case Target[Epi].

The initial mass or circulating pool is the Target[Epi]
multiplied by the initial volume of distribution, in
this case the ECFV.

Initial Mass = Target[Epi]*ECFV.InitialVol(L)

</content>
