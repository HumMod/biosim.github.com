<topicdoc>
<name> Gluconeogenesis - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

The liver plays a key role in the maintenance of blood glucose
homeostasis. It releases glucose in the fasted state and takes
up and stores some of the glucose ingested in meals. In doing
so the liver ensures an even and predictable supply of glucose
to the extrahepatic tissues, primarily the brain.

The liver is able to release glucose to the circulation from
its glycogen stores (glycogenolysis) and it has the capacity
to take up the 3-carbon fragments lactate, pyruvate, glycerol
and amino acids and convert these to glucose. The latter process
is called gluconeogenesis.

In the overnight fasted state, the liver is the main source of
glucose production and it is widely believed that glycogenolysis
and gluconeogenesis each contribute approximately 50% of glucose
turnover in healthy subjects.

During continued fasting,gluconeogenesis from both the kidneys
and the liver account for almost all glucose production.

Ekberg K, Landau BR, Wajngot A, Chandramouli V, Efendic S,
Brunengraber H, Wahren J. Contributions by kidney and liver to
glucose production in the postabsorptive state and after 60 h of
fasting. Diabetes 48:292 - 298, 1999.

Wahren J, Ekberg K. Splanchnic regulation of glucose production
Ann Rev Nutr 27: 329 - 345, 2007.

Chandramouli V, Ekberg K, Schumann WC, Kalhan SC, Wahren J, Landau BR.
Quantifying gluconeogenesis during fasting.
Am J Physiol Endocinol Metab 273: E1209 - E1215, 1997.

Kruszynska YT. Normal metabolism: the physiology of fuel
homeostasis. Chapter 9, Textbook of Diabetes edited by
Pickup JC & Williams G. 2003 Blackwell Science

<topicdoc>
<name> Glucose Effect - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Glucose has the ability to modulate its own metabolism by
the liver, independent of any change in insulin concentration.
When insulin and glucagon are at basal levels, hyperglycemia
inhibits endogenous glucose output and increases tissue glucose
uptake.

DOG STUDIES
Shulman GI, Liljenquist JE, Williams PE, Lacy WW, Cherrington AD.
Glucose disposal during insulinopenia in somatostatin-treated
dogs. The roles of glucose and glucagon.
J Clin Invest 62: 487 - 491, 1978

HUMAN STUDIES
Sacca L, Hendler R, Sherwin RS.
Hyperglycemia inhibits glucose production in man independent of
changes in glucoregulatory hormones.
J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 1978 Nov;47(5):1160-3.

Liljenquist JE, Mueller GL, Cherrington AD, Perry JM, Rabinowitz D.
Hyperglycemia per se (insulin and glucagon withdrawn) can inhibit
hepatic glucose production in man.
J Clin Endocrinol Metab 48:171 - 175, 1979

DeFronzo RA, Ferrannini E, Hendler R, Felig P, Wahren J.
Regulation of splanchnic and peripheral glucose uptake by insulin
and hyperglycemia in man. Diabetes 32: 35 - 45, 1983.

RAT STUDIES
Ruderman NB, Herrera MG. Glucose regulation of hepatic
gluconeogenesis. Am J Physiol 214: 1346 - 1351, 1968

</content>
</topicdoc>

<name> Insulin Effect - Overview </name>
<label> Overview </label>
<content>

Substantial increases in insulin levels are required for
inhibition of gluconeogenesis.

HUMAN STUDIES
DeFronzo RA, Ferrannini E, Hendler R, Felig P, Wahren J.
Regulation of splanchnic and peripheral glucose uptake by
insulin and hyperglycemia in man. Diabetes 32: 35 - 45, 1983.

Felig P, Wahren J. Influence of endogenous insulin secretion
on splanchnic glucose and amino acid metabolism in man.
J Clin Invest 50: 1702 - 1711, 1971

DOGS
Chiasson J, Liljenquist J, Finger F, Lacy W. Differential
sensitivity of glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis to insulin
infusion in dogs. Diabetes 25: 283 - 291, 1976.
